Chapter 1 — Project Topic Selection and Explanation 
1.1  What Is This Project? 
This project is a community-based housing discovery and review platform built for foreigners 
and young people living in Chinese cities. Users can browse affordable housing listings, read 
honest reviews from other tenants, and rate neighborhoods based on their real living experience. 
The platform is built using Java J2EE technologies with a MySQL database backend. 
1.2  What Problem Does It Address? 
China is currently going through one of the worst property crises in its history. For roughly two 
decades, real estate was treated as the safest and most profitable investment in the country. 
Developers borrowed enormous amounts of money to build as fast a s possible, and ordinary 
families poured their savings into apartments — not necessarily to live in, but because they 
believed property prices would always go up. 
That belief has now broken. Major developers like Evergrande have collapsed under hundreds 
of billions of dollars in debt. Across the country, there are an estimated 65 to 80 million empty 
apartments that nobody is living in. Entire districts were built faster than the actual population 
needed them, and in many cities, new residential areas stand almost completely empty — what 
people now call "ghost cities." 
  
(News coverage of China's property crisis. Source: X, 微博 )

The crisis is made worse by two deeper issues. First, China's birth rate has been falling for 
years, which means there are fewer young families forming and therefore less natural demand 
for new housing. Second, many people who could afford to buy are simpl y choosing not to , 
because they are afraid prices will keep dropping. When enough people think that way, it 
becomes a self-fulfilling cycle: less buying, less confidence, lower prices, even less buying. 
 
 
 
(Empty residential buildings in China's ghost cities. Source: Google, 微博) 
The result is a strange and painful situation. There are millions of empty apartments across 
China. At the same time, many people — especially young workers, migrants, and international 
students,are struggling to find affordable and trustworthy housing in the cities where they 
actually want to live. The supply exists. But the connection between available housing and the 
people who need it is broken. 
A big part of why that connection is broken is trust. Most existing Chinese housing platforms 
are flooded with developer advertisements and inflated listings. There is very little honest, 
community-sourced information about what it is actually like to live in a specific apartment or 
neighborhood — especially from the perspective of foreigners, who face additional challenges 
like language barriers and unfamiliarity with local systems. Many international students end 
up finding housing through informal WeChat groups or by relying on whoever happens to help 
them when they arrive, rather than through any organized or reliable source of information. 
This platform is built to address that specific gap. It is not trying to fix the entire property crisis 
— that would be unrealistic. But it aims to make one part of the problem smaller: helping 
people who genuinely need housing find, evaluate, and trust th eir options through honest 
community reviews and neighborhood ratings.

1.3  Why Is This Topic Worth Building? 
 
It solves a real problem. 
The housing information gap described above is happening right now. There is a clear group 
of people — foreign students, young migrants, first -time renters — who do not have a good 
way to find trusted housing information in China. This platform directly serves that group. 
It is realistic to build. 
The system does not require external APIs or complex integrations. All of its core features — 
user registration, login, housing listings, reviews, ratings, and neighborhood pages — can be 
built entirely with the technologies covered in this course, includi ng Servlet, JSP, EL 
expressions, JSTL, JDBC, Filter, Session and Cookie management, and the five -tier 
architecture pattern with a MySQL database. 
It has genuine social value. 
By helping people navigate the housing market more confidently, the platform contributes — 
even in a small way — toward getting some of China's vacant housing stock into actual use. 
Every person who finds a good apartment through honest community information is one fewer 
empty unit sitting idle.

It is personally relevant. 
This project was inspired by my own personal experience. As an international student living in 
Nanjing, I have seen firsthand how difficult it is for foreigners to find reliable housing 
information without local connections or language support. When I visi ted Shenzhen, I was 
struck by how many fully built apartment buildings and commercial areas were standing almost 
completely empty — a visible reminder of just how real this crisis is. That experience is what 
gave this project its direction and purpose. 
1.4  Research Orientation 
This project sits at the intersection of two things: understanding a real social and economic 
problem, and applying Java development skills to build something that responds to it. The goal 
is to think through how software can be designed to serve people who are often left out of 
existing platforms — in this case, foreigners trying to find their footing in a Chinese city during 
a difficult period in the country's housing market. 
The project also serves as a full demonstration of the J2EE technology stack studied throughout 
this course, from basic HTTP and Servlet fundamentals through to layered backend architecture 
and modern Spring Boot controller design. The aim is to build some thing that works well, 
makes sense as a product, and reflects the kind of thinking that goes into real -world web 
development.

Chapter 2 — Research and Analysis of Similar Projects 
2.1  Overview 
To better understand what already exists in the housing platform space and where the gaps are, 
three well-known platforms were researched and analyzed: Anjuke ( 安居客), Lianjia (链家), 
and Trulia. These three were chosen because they are the most relevant comparisons to this 
project — two from China and one from the West — and together they show both what current 
solutions offer and what they are still missing. 
2.2  Anjuke (安居客) 
What It Is 
Anjuke is one of China's biggest online real estate platforms, founded in 2007 and now owned 
by 58.com. It covers new homes, second -hand homes, rental properties, and commercial real 
estate all in one place. It is primarily used by Chinese buyers, renters, and real estate agents. 
 
(Anjuke (安居客) website homepage and Wechat mini program) 
 
Architectural Model 
Anjuke most likely follows a standard MVC (Model-View-Controller) architecture at its core, 
with a clear separation between the data layer, business logic, and front-end presentation. Given 
its scale, it likely uses a microservices-based backend where different services handle listings, 
search, user management, and payments independently. The front end is mobile -first, built as 
a native app with a web platform running in parallel.

Core Features 
• Millions of property listings searchable by city, price range, property type, and size 
• High-resolution photos, floor plans, and detailed descriptions for each listing 
• Price trend data and market analysis tools 
• VR home viewing and 3D tours for new developments 
• In-app messaging between users and landlords or agents 
• Mobile-first design with a widely used Android and iOS app 
What It Does Well 
Anjuke is very strong at aggregating a huge volume of listings in one place and making them 
easy to filter and browse. The search experience is smooth, and the amount of data available 
— price history, area comparisons, neighborhood maps — is genuinely use ful for someone 
who already knows what they are looking for. 
What It Is Missing 
The biggest weakness of Anjuke is that it is entirely driven by advertisers and real estate agents. 
Almost all the content on the platform comes from developers and agents trying to sell or rent 
properties, which means the information is naturally biased. There are no genuine community 
reviews from tenants who have actually lived in these apartments. The platform is also entirely 
in Chinese, with no English or multilingual support — making it essentially unusable for 
international students and foreign workers without strong Mandarin skills. 
2.3  Lianjia (链家) 
What It Is 
Lianjia was founded in 2001 in Beijing and has grown into China's leading real estate brokerage 
brand. Unlike Anjuke, which is primarily a listings platform, Lianjia combines online listings 
with an offline network of physical stores and trained agents.

Architectural Model 
Lianjia most likely uses an Online-to-Offline (O2O) architecture, meaning its digital platform 
is tightly integrated with its physical branch network. The backend follows a layered 
architecture similar to the five -tier model — with a presentation layer, business logic layer, 
data access layer, and database — extended with real-time agent coordination tools. Its parent 
platform Beike (贝壳) acts as the broader ecosystem layer on top. 
Core Features 
• Verified property listings confirmed through agent inspection and ownership checks 
• A large offline network of physical branches across major Chinese cities 
• An Agent Cooperation Network (ACN) that allows agents to share commissions 
• Transaction support tools including deposit guarantees and fund safekeeping 
• Price trend data and market insights 
What It Does Well 
Lianjia's main strength is trust and verification. Unlike most listing platforms where anyone 
can post anything, Lianjia has agents who physically inspect properties and verify ownership 
before listings go live. Their transaction support features — like pr otecting deposits and 
ensuring money is held safely during deals — also give users more confidence when making a 
major purchase.

What It Is Missing 
Lianjia is built around the buying and selling process, with professional agents at the center of 
every interaction. This makes it less useful for ordinary renters who just want to find a decent 
apartment. Like Anjuke, there is no space for community-sourced information. Tenants cannot 
leave reviews about landlord behavior, an apartment's actual condition, or what daily life in a 
neighborhood feels like. It is also entirely built for Chinese users, with no English interface or 
foreigner-specific guidance. 
2.4  Trulia (United States) 
What It Is 
Trulia is the largest real estate platform in the United States, launched in 2006. While it operates 
in a completely different market from China, it is the most relevant international reference for 
this project because it introduced community-based features into the housing search experience 
— something that does not yet exist in China's housing platforms. 
 
(Trulia website listings page) 
Architectural Model 
Trulia is also most likely uses a modern web architecture based on MVC principles, with a 
React-based frontend communicating with RESTful backend services. At the data layer, it 
combines a relational database for structured listing data with big data proce ssing tools for its 
Zestimate pricing algorithm. Zillow Group’s acquisition of Trulia means the neighborhood 
review features are served from a separate but integrated service.

Core Features 
• A large searchable database of homes for sale, rent, and recently sold 
• "Zestimate" — an AI-generated estimate of a property's current market value 
• Interactive map-based search with filters for schools, transport, and nearby amenities 
• Neighborhood insights including crime data, school ratings, and walkability scores 
• User reviews from residents about their neighborhoods 
• A community Q&A section where users can ask questions about specific areas 
What It Does Well 
Trulia’s most distinctive contribution is that it treats housing search as more than just a 
transaction. Through neighborhood ratings, resident reviews, and community Q&A features, it 
gives users a way to understand what it actually feels like to live some where — not just what 
the listing says. This is the community layer that makes the platform significantly more useful 
than a simple listings database. 
What It Is Missing 
Trulia was built for the American market and has no presence or relevance in China. Its 
community review model — which is genuinely valuable — does not exist in any Chinese 
housing platform. It also does not specifically address the needs of foreigners or international 
users trying to navigate a new country.

Chapter 3 — Detailed Design of Personal Project Modules and Functions 
3.1  Chosen Architecture Model 
This project follows the Five -Tier Architecture pattern, which is the standard model taught 
throughout this course. The five tiers are : 
• View Layer (JSP / Thymeleaf) : the pages the user sees and interacts with in the browser 
• Controller Layer (Servlet) : receives requests from the browser and decides what to do 
with them 
• Service Layer : handles all the business logic, validation, and rules 
• DAO Layer (Data Access Object) : handles all direct communication with the database 
• Database Layer (MySQL) : stores all the data permanently 
This model is also consistent with the MVC (Model-View-Controller) design pattern, which is 
one of the most widely used patterns in web development. In MVC terms, the JSP pages act as 
the View, the Servlet acts as the Controller, and the Service + DAO layers together form the 
Model. Entity classes serve as data carriers across all layers. 
For one module (the Neighborhood Ratings feature), the project also uses Spring Boot with 
Thymeleaf as a modern alternative to JSP, demonstrating the full range of technologies covered 
in this course. 
The execution flow for every user action follows this path: 
JSP Page → Servlet → Service → DAO → MySQL → back up the same chain

3.3  Module 1 — User Module 
Overview 
The User Module handles everything related to user identity — registering a new account, 
logging in, maintaining login state across pages, and logging out. This is the entry point for all 
other features since most actions on the platform require the user to be logged in. 
Core Functions 
• User Registration — new users create an account with username, email, password, 
nationality, and city 
• User Login — registered users log in with their username and password 
• Remember Me — users can stay logged in across browser sessions using a Cookie 
• Login State Management — the user's identity is stored in a Session after login 
• Logout — destroys the Session and clears the Remember Me Cookie 
• Login Interception — a Filter redirects unauthenticated users to the login page 
 
 


========== PAGE 15 ==========
15 
 
Implementation Logic 
Registration uses a RegisterServlet that reads form parameters, calls UserService to validate 
input and check for duplicate usernames, then calls UserDAO to insert the new user into the 
database. Login uses a LoginServlet that verifies credentials through UserService and 
UserDAO, then stores the user object in the HttpSession. The Remember Me feature sets a 
Cookie with a 7-day expiry. A LoginFilter mapped to /* checks every incoming request for a 
valid Session. Logout is handled by LogoutServlet which calls session.invalidate() and sets the 
Cookie maxAge to 0. 
Key Technologies 
Servlet, HttpSession, Cookie, Filter, FilterChain, JSP, JDBC, DAO pattern, five-tier 
architecture 
Database Table 
user (id, username, email, password, nationality, city, role, created_at)  
 
3.4  Module 2 — Housing Listings Module 
Overview 
The Housing Listings Module is the core browsing experience. It allows users to view available 
housing listings, search and filter by city, price range, and property type, and view the full 
details of any listing they are interested in. 
Core Functions 
• Browse Listings — paginated list of all available housing listings 
• Search and Filter — filter by city, price range, and property type 
• Listing Detail Page — full details including description, address, price, and average 
tenant rating 
• Submit a Listing — registered users can submit a new housing listing 
• My Listings — users can view and manage their own submitted listings 
Implementation Logic 
ListingServlet handles GET requests, reads filter parameters using getParameter(), and passes 
them to ListingService. ListingService validates the filter inputs and calls ListingDAO with 
the appropriate query parameters. ListingDAO builds a dynamic SQL que ry and returns a list 
of Listing entity objects. The JSP view uses JSTL c:forEach to loop through the list and EL

3.5  Module 3 — Reviews and Ratings Module 
Overview 
This is the most distinctive module of the platform. It allows tenants who have lived in a 
property to leave an honest written review and a star rating (1 to 5). This community -sourced 
layer is what separates this platform from existing Chinese housing apps. 
Core Functions 
• Submit a Review — logged-in users write a review and give a star rating for any 
listing 
• View Reviews — all reviews for a listing are displayed on its detail page, newest first 
• Average Rating Display — each listing shows an automatically calculated average 
star rating 
• One Review Per User Per Listing — prevents spam and duplicate reviews 
• Edit or Delete Own Review — users can update or remove their own reviews 
Implementation Logic 
ReviewServlet handles both GET (load reviews) and POST (submit review) requests. On POST, 
it reads listing_id, rating, and review_text from the request, calls ReviewService to validate, 
then ReviewDAO to insert. The average rating is calculated using SQL A VG(rating) grouped 
by listing_id. The one-review-per-user check is enforced in ReviewService before allowing a 
new submission. JSP displays reviews using c:forEach and shows star ratings using EL 
expressions. 
Key Technologies 
Servlet (doGet, doPost), JSP, EL, JSTL, JDBC, DAO, SQL AVG() aggregation, Session for 
user identity check

3.6  Module 4 — Neighborhood Module 
Overview 
The Neighborhood Module gives users a broader view of the areas where listings are located. 
Instead of just evaluating a single apartment, users can explore an entire neighborhood — 
reading community tips, checking liveability scores, and understanding wha t daily life in that 
area is actually like. This module is implemented using Spring Boot and Thymeleaf. 
Core Functions 
• Neighborhood Profile Page — dedicated page per neighborhood with name, city, 
description, and liveability score 
• Liveability Ratings — users rate a neighborhood across four categories: safety, 
transport, food access, and foreigner-friendliness 
• Community Tips — users post short text tips about a neighborhood 
• Listings in This Neighborhood — each neighborhood page also shows all related 
housing listings 
Implementation Logic 
This module is built as a Spring Boot application with a NeighborhoodController using 
@GetMapping and @PostMapping annotations. The controller calls NeighborhoodService, 
which calls NeighborhoodRepository (DAO) to fetch data from MySQL. Thymeleaf templates 
use th:each to loop through listings and tips, and th:text to display values. Liveability scores 
are averaged across all user submissions per category. 
Key Technologies 
Spring Boot, @Controller, @GetMapping, @PostMapping, Model, Thymeleaf (th:each, 
th:text, th:action), JDBC, DAO pattern 
Database Tables 
neighborhood (id, name, city, description) 
neighborhood_rating (id, neighborhood_id, user_id, safety, transport, 
food_access, foreigner_friendly) 
neighborhood_tip (id, neighborhood_id, user_id, tip_text, created_at)

3.7  Module 5 — Admin Module 
Overview 
The Admin Module gives a designated administrator account the ability to manage the 
platform's content — approving or removing listings, moderating reviews, and managing user 
accounts. This ensures the platform stays clean and trustworthy for all users. 
Core Functions 
• Admin Login — admin is identified by a role flag in the database and redirected to the 
admin dashboard after login 
• Manage Listings — view all listings, approve pending ones, edit details, or delete 
inappropriate ones 
• Moderate Reviews — view all reviews and delete any that violate community 
guidelines 
• Manage Users — view all registered users and deactivate abusive accounts 
• Dashboard Overview — shows key statistics: total users, listings, reviews, and pending 
listings 
Implementation Logic 
Admin identity is stored in the user table with a role column set to "admin". After login, 
LoginServlet checks the role and redirects to /admin/dashboard instead of the regular 
homepage. An AdminFilter is mapped to /admin/* and checks that the current session user has 
the admin role — any non -admin accessing /admin/* is immediately redirected. 
AdminDashboardServlet queries summary counts and passes them to the dashboard JSP via 
request.setAttribute(). Each admin management Servlet handles GET (list view) an d POST 
(actions: delete, approve, deactivate) handlers. 
Key Technologies 
Servlet, AdminFilter on /admin/*, HttpSession role check, JSP, JSTL, EL, JDBC, DAO, five -
tier architecture, request.setAttribute(), request forwarding 
Database Addition 
role VARCHAR(20) DEFAULT 'user'  — added to user table 
Admin account seeded manually with role = 'admin'
